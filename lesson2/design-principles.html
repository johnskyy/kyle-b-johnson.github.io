Visual Hierarchy - Herman Miller, Inc.

Herman Miller is a furniture company that caters to professional, office-oriented customers. As such, they pride themselves on having modern, ergonomic designs. They are also a business so they are naturally going to have desired user interactions or products that they want to highlight. These diesired pages and outcomes are highlighted on the home page. Additionally, as products are viewed, the aspects of the product that the marketing team (or others) want to highlight are going to be more prominent on the respective webapge and take up a larger portion of the viewport's real estate.

Rule of Thirds - SFMOMA

The San Francisco Museum of Modern Art (SFMOMA) houses a large collection of Modern and Contemporary art pieces, as such, they have a fairly design-forward website. One such example is their visitor's guide as linked above. This page has a fairly large amount of information but they manage to keep the top banner image, and most information tiles to about 1/3 of the screen size and the main information tile comprises about 2/3 of the viewport while it is within the viewport.

White Space and Clean Design - Grød

Grød is a small chain of porridge restaurants in Denmark. Their product maybe fairly simple, but they put an emphasis on clean tastes and flavors and they employ a website that mirrors that philosophy. They manage to organize the page so that there is minimal "fluff" which allows for the images to speak for themselves as well as creating just enough space for the next item in the page to creep into view providing just enough information to intrigue the user.
